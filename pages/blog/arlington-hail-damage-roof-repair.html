Hail Damage Roof Repair in Arlington, TX: What Every Homeowner Needs to Know

From identifying storm damage to filing an insurance claim and finding a trustworthy contractor — a complete guide for Arlington homeowners.

March 16, 2026 SkyGuard Roofing Solutions Roofing

Arlington, Texas occupies a unique and challenging position in the American weather landscape. Situated in the heart of Tornado Alley between Dallas and Fort Worth, the city experiences some of the most severe spring and early summer weather in the country. Hailstorms that track through Tarrant County can deposit stones ranging from pea-sized to baseball-sized, and the damage to residential roofing can range from cosmetic granule loss to full structural compromise of the roof deck. Understanding what to do in the hours and days following a hail event is critical to protecting both your home and your insurance claim. This guide walks Arlington homeowners through every stage of the process.

Arlington's Position in Tornado Alley

The term "Tornado Alley" has been used for decades to describe the corridor from Texas through Oklahoma and Kansas where atmospheric conditions regularly produce severe convective storms. Arlington and the broader DFW metroplex are no strangers to these events. In a typical year, the National Weather Service issues multiple severe thunderstorm and tornado warnings for Tarrant County between March and June, with significant events also occurring in the fall transitional season. Many of these storms produce hail as a standard byproduct, and even storms that do not reach tornado intensity can produce damaging hail for 30 to 40 minutes as they move across the region.

For Arlington homeowners, this is not a question of if a hailstorm will damage your roof — it is a question of when, and whether you are prepared to respond effectively. A roof that appears undamaged to the naked eye from the ground can have significant shingle bruising, granule displacement, and compromised seals that will accelerate deterioration over the next two to three years if not addressed.

How to Identify Hail Damage on Your Roof

Hail damage is not always obvious. Large hailstones may crack tiles or puncture membranes in ways that are visible, but the more common scenario with asphalt shingles is subtler. Here is what to look for during an initial assessment, either from the ground with binoculars or from a safe ladder position at the eave line.

Signs of Hail Damage on Asphalt Shingles

Hail strikes on asphalt shingles typically appear as circular or irregular impact marks where the granule surface has been disturbed or displaced. In the immediate aftermath of a storm, these marks may look dark and wet. As they dry, they reveal a matte, rough texture compared to the surrounding granule surface. Over time, these exposed areas accelerate UV degradation and moisture absorption, shortening the shingle's effective life significantly. You may also notice granule accumulation in gutters and downspout splash areas — a normal amount after heavy rain, but excessive following a hailstorm indicates significant granule loss from the shingles.

Check soft metals on the exterior for additional evidence: HVAC units, gutters, downspouts, vents, and flashing are excellent indicators of hail size and intensity. Dents in these surfaces correspond to the impacts your roof experienced. The pattern and density of denting helps a professional assess the severity of the event.

Signs of Hail Damage on Other Roofing Materials

Metal roofing typically shows small circular dents without penetrating the surface. For most storm intensities, metal roofing remains functional after hail events, though cosmetic damage may be present. Clay or concrete tile can crack or spall, with cracked tiles often requiring individual replacement. Flat roof membranes should be inspected carefully for punctures, splits at seams, and areas where the membrane has separated from the substrate.

When to Call for a Professional Inspection

If your area was included in a severe thunderstorm warning or your neighbors are reporting damage, schedule a professional inspection regardless of what you can see from the ground. A trained inspector will get on the roof, document damage systematically with photographs and measurements, and provide a written report that supports your insurance claim. Attempting to access the roof yourself after a storm carries safety risks and can inadvertently create additional damage or compromise evidence needed for your claim.

Navigating the Insurance Claim Process

Filing a homeowner's insurance claim for hail damage is a multi-step process that rewards preparation and documentation. Here is how to approach it effectively.

Step 1: Document Everything Before Calling Your Insurer

Before you contact your insurance company, gather as much evidence as possible. Photograph all damage you can safely access — gutters, vents, siding, windows, vehicles. Note the date and time of the storm and check local weather records or storm reports from the National Weather Service for official confirmation of the event. This information supports your claim by establishing the timeline and severity of the weather event.

Step 2: Contact Your Insurance Company Promptly

Most homeowner's policies require that you report damage within a reasonable timeframe — often 30 to 60 days of the event, though this varies by policy. Contact your insurer's claims department to open a claim. An adjuster will be assigned and will schedule a visit to inspect the property. You have the right to have your own contractor present during the adjuster's inspection, which we strongly recommend.

Step 3: Review the Adjuster's Scope

The insurance adjuster's scope of loss document outlines what your insurer agrees to pay for. Review it carefully against the inspection report from your contractor. If there are discrepancies — items the adjuster missed, undervalued line items, or code upgrade requirements not included — your contractor can help you submit a supplement to address those gaps. Supplementing insurance claims is a normal part of the process; do not accept the initial scope as final if it does not accurately reflect the full extent of the damage.

Step 4: Temporary Repairs and Preventing Further Damage

If the storm has left areas of the roof exposed or created active leaks, you have a responsibility under your policy to prevent further damage to the home's interior. Temporary tarping and emergency repairs are typically covered under most policies as part of the overall claim. Document any temporary repairs with photographs and keep receipts for materials. Choosing a Contractor in Arlington: Avoiding Storm Chasers

In the days following a significant hail event in Arlington, out-of-state roofing crews — commonly called storm chasers — will begin canvassing neighborhoods offering quick inspections and same-day estimates. While not all traveling contractors are disreputable, the storm-chasing business model creates inherent problems for homeowners. These companies are often unavailable for warranty claims once they leave the area, may use lower-quality materials to maximize margins, and in some cases engage in fraudulent practices involving inflated damage claims or signing over insurance benefits without the homeowner's full understanding.

The safest approach is to work with an established local Arlington roofing contractor with a verifiable physical address, state licensing, and a track record in the community. Ask for the contractor's Texas Roofing Contractor license number, proof of general liability insurance (minimum $1 million per occurrence), and workers' compensation coverage. Verify the license through the Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation. Request references from recent local customers and take the time to check them. A contractor who resists providing any of this information is not the right choice for your home.

Timeline for Hail Damage Repairs in Arlington

After a widespread hail event across the DFW area, contractor schedules fill quickly. Expect timelines from initial inspection to completed repair to range from two to eight weeks, depending on the severity and geographic scope of the storm and the volume of claims in process. Material lead times can also extend timelines if specific products are in high demand region-wide. Once the insurance claim is approved and a scope agreed upon, an experienced contractor should be able to complete a typical residential reroof in one to two days.
